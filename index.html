Exploring drug-related crimes in the City by the Bay - San Francisco

This page presents a small visual story built from historical San Francisco police incident reports. Use it to explore the spatial and temporal patterns of drug-crimes in the city, and to get a sense of how data visualization can help us understand complex social phenomena.

Note on Interpretation: These visualizations represent police incident reports, not necessarily the total volume of drug use or crime. Shifts in trends may reflect changes in SFPD enforcement priorities, city policy, or reporting protocols rather than a change in underlying activity.
Where it started "Accidental drug overdose deaths began to spike in the mid- to late 2010s as fentanyl infiltrated the local drug market." (SF Chronicle, 2026)
The impact "Local heroes, artists, neighbors, friends, and relatives lost their lives. In the face of so much death, public health experts were befuddled, and political careers crumbled." (SF Standard, 2026)
How it's going "In 2024, the San Francisco Office of the Chief Medical Examiner reported that 635 people died from an unintentional drug overdose in San Francisco." (SF Government)

Police districts in San Francisco

This analysis is organized by SFPD district. District names reflect official reporting areas plus two non-district categories used in the dataset.

  • Taraval — Southwestern neighborhoods, including Outer Sunset and Parkside areas.
  • Ingleside — Southern residential corridor around Excelsior, Oceanview, and nearby zones.
  • Northern — North-central area including parts of Polk Gulch, Pacific Heights, and Civic-adjacent blocks.
  • Southern — Downtown/South of Market corridor with dense commercial and transit activity.
  • Mission — Mission District and surrounding inner-east neighborhoods.
  • Richmond — Northwest side, including Inner/Outer Richmond near Golden Gate Park’s north edge.
  • Central — Historic core, including Financial District, Chinatown, and Union Square vicinity.
  • Bayview — Southeastern industrial/residential area, including Bayview–Hunters Point.
  • Tenderloin — High-density central neighborhood with concentrated service and nightlife activity.
  • Park — Central-west district around Golden Gate Park and nearby neighborhoods.
  • Out of SF — Incidents recorded outside San Francisco boundaries.
  • Unknown — Missing district value (NaN) in the source records.

Visualizations

The following visualizations explore drug-related crime patterns in San Francisco across time and space. Each plot is designed to highlight different aspects of the data, from long-term trends to spatial distributions and hourly patterns.

Static monthly drug trends

Long-term trends in drug-related incidents across San Francisco. The plot shows the number of incidents per month over time. The plot is a tribute to how much the drug landscape has changed in the city, from the 2000s with ~800 incidents per month to present day with ~300 incidents per day. The 2010s show a clear downward trend symbolizing how the city was able to fight the drug epidemic. Around March 2020, the COVID-19 pandemic hit, and the city went into lockdown. This is reflected in the plot with a sharp drop in drug-related incidents, as the city was empty and the police were focused on enforcing lockdown measures. However, the later 2020s show an alarming upward trend, reflecting the rise of fentanyl and the ongoing challenges in addressing drug-related issues in San Francisco.

Temporal heatmap of drug crimes in San Francisco

A temporal heatmap showing both the spatial and temporal distribution of drug-related crimes in San Francisco. The heatmap uses intensity of color to visualize when and where the drug-related crimes happened. Looking at the animation frames sliding across the screen it becomes apparent that not only are the drug-related crimes centered around down-town San Francisco. Furthermore, we see the decrease in incident reports as the heatmap dissipates and becomes scarce. Similar to the static plot in the above card. While the heatmap is heavily saturated in the Tenderloin district, this density may be a function of proactive policing rather than a unique concentration of activity. As a high-priority zone for the SFPD, the Tenderloin experiences significantly higher patrol frequency and surveillance, which naturally leads to a higher volume of incident reports. As stated in SF Planning report, "Moreover, the neighborhood accounted for nearly 50% of the city's drug offense police reports, ..." (SF Planning, 2024), which is a clear indication of the disproportionate focus on this district.

The shifting hotspots in these heatmaps do not necessarily indicate the successful eradication of crime in specific neighborhoods. Instead, they likely reflect spatial displacement or changes in proactive policing strategies, where law enforcement shifts its focus and patrols to different areas. Furthermore, it is critical to acknowledge the role of selection bias in this dataset. As these figures are derived solely from police incident reports, they only capture crimes that were either observed by officers or reported by the public. This creates a "dark figure" of drug activity, meaning offenses that occur in private or in unpatrolled areas remain invisible to the data. Consequently, these heatmaps are more accurately described as a visualization of police activity and public reporting density rather than a comprehensive map of all drug-related occurrences in San Francisco.

Interactive hourly profile

This next plot gives a different perspective. The plot shows a cumulative time of day for incident reports, separated by year. It is very apparent that the most drug-related crimes are caught in the afternoon hours, with a peak around 3 pm. This could be the result of a higher police activity in the afternoon, along with better visibility and more people being around to report drug-related crimes. On the opposite end of the scale, the low time is at 5 am, where fewer people are around and the police activity is lower. Keep in mind that these numbers are unlikely to match the exact distribution of drug use, as an entry in this data set requires the incident to be reported. Furthermore, the various traces in the plot are purposefully left blank. Take your time to discover the trends and insights hidden. You can select the years from early 2000s to 2021 to see the massive fall and clean-up done in the drug landscape in San Francisco. Afterwards, select the years from 2021 to present to see the recent rise in drug-related incidents as San Francisco is facing the fentanyl epidemic.

Method & context

The visualizations are based on historical SFPD incident-report data. The goal is descriptive insight: where incidents cluster, when activity peaks, and how broad trends evolve through time.
